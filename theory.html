Geometric foundations

Theory

A local geometric constraint on a fiber bundle, from which relativistic kinematics is reconstructed rather than postulated.

1. Two separate theories, patched together

Special relativity and quantum mechanics each treat mass and energy as distinct entities. E=mc² links them numerically, but doesn't explain why they are two aspects of one thing. The Planck relation (E=ℏω) and the de Broglie relation (p=ℏk) are likewise introduced as two independent postulates, not derived from a shared origin. To make relativistic observations consistent, the standard approach bends spacetime itself — the Minkowski metric and the Lorentz transformations are taken as primitive, rather than justified from anything more fundamental.

2. Massless vs. massive: a question of locality

The real difference between a massive particle and a photon isn't really about "having mass" as a property — it's about locality for a massive observer. A massive object can, in principle, sit still in front of you. Light never can: it always propagates at c, for every observer, without exception. Beta starts from asking what geometric fact could produce exactly this distinction.

3. One vibration, always at c

The core idea: there is a single vibration, propagating isotropically at speed c on a trivial fiber bundle. When this wave's propagation sits entirely or partly in an internal, non-observable dimension, it appears — to a massive observer using their own internal clock — as localized, at rest, or slower than light. Nothing ever actually moves slower than c; what changes is how much of that motion is visible to a given observer.

3.1 The bundle, the two postulates, and internal time

The bundle is M = D¹ × S¹: a one-dimensional observable base and a compact internal circular fiber of fixed circumference. Two postulates fix everything that follows. Postulate 1 is a local dispersion relation: the wave's frequency is fixed by its wave-vector components along the base and the fiber combined, so that both group and phase velocity along the wave's own direction equal c. Postulate 2 restricts the internal fiber to its fundamental standing-wave mode. A particle's own proper time is simply the period of its own internal rotation on the fiber — and rest mass is the energy of that internal rotation alone, divided by c². See why D¹ × S¹ → for the full reasoning behind this choice of structure, and time as an internal clock → for what this means for simultaneity and dilation.

3.2 Kinematics as partial observation

Relativistic kinematics emerges because a massive observer, using their own internal clock, only ever measures part of this wave's total motion — specifically, the part that runs along the observable base direction. That visible portion shows up as translational velocity and relativistic kinetic energy. The rest of the motion, confined to the internal fiber, is what gets perceived as rest mass. Nothing is added or hidden — the same fixed total is simply split differently depending on how much of it moves observably.

4. Everything follows from this one scheme

A trivial bundle, a single wave propagating isotropically at constant speed, and observers who are never privileged — each measuring only with their own internal clock. From just this geometric scheme, the full set of relativistic kinematical observables emerges: time dilation, relativistic momentum, the energy-momentum relation, the constancy of light propagation between moving observers, and the composition of velocities.

5. Lorentz transformations as the only consistent option

Lorentz invariance is not assumed at the outset. It arises as an emergent consistency structure: when different observers, each using their own internal clock as time parameter, must give mutually coherent descriptions of the same constrained bundle, the standard inertial-frame assumptions (linearity, homogeneity, reciprocity) select Lorentz invariance as the unique symmetry that keeps those descriptions consistent with one another.

6. One definition of energy, two quantum relations

Energy is defined operationally, once: it is the rate at which wavefronts cross a fixed point, multiplied by ℏ. Applied directly, this yields the Planck relation. Decomposing the same geometric dispersion algebraically along the bundle's two directions then yields the de Broglie relation as well — both quantum relations, from a single measurement rule, rather than two separate postulates.

7. Two readings of the same energy

In a particle's own proper frame, only the internal wavefronts exist — this gives the ontological energy content, m₀c², fixed by the geometry of the internal fiber alone. An observer for whom the particle is moving sees a different wavefront rate, combining the (dilated) internal rotation with the translational motion — this gives the observational energy, γm₀c². Combining both registers reproduces the standard relativistic dispersion relation, E² = (pₓc)² + (m₀c²)², as a geometric identity on the bundle rather than an assumed formula. See energy and mass → for the full picture, including how a photon exchanging energy with a massive particle is constrained by this same structure (manuscript §5.8).

8. Inertial mass: rigidity meets scaling

Inertial mass has a twofold origin here. First, the internal wave-vector is topologically rigid — fixed by the fiber's circumference, and no ordinary interaction can redistribute energy into it. Second, the ontological content is invariant, so producing a larger observable effect as velocity increases requires an ever-larger γ factor. Neither ingredient alone produces inertia — it's the interplay between a quantity that cannot change (the internal rigidity) and a scaling factor that must grow without bound as v → c that shows up, phenomenologically, as resistance to acceleration (manuscript §5.9).

9. The dispersion cone: one object, two cuts

The circle used throughout this site (rest energy, radius 1) and the hyperbola of the relativistic energy-momentum relation are not two separate constructions — they are two different cuts through the same underlying object: the dispersion cone defined by Postulate 1. Slicing the cone at fixed frequency gives the circular, Euclidean partition between internal and translational motion. Slicing it at fixed internal wave-number gives the hyperbolic mass shell. The two slices are connected by the same relation that appears everywhere else on this site: γ = sec α = cosh φ.
